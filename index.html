How the Exam is Split

PART A
80 marks, 80 MCQs — Reasoning, Maths, Constitution of India, Current Affairs & GK. You need at least 32 marks (40%) to clear this part. This is the same for all PSI exams, so lots of practice material exists online.
PART B
120 marks, 120 MCQs — Electronics, Communication, Computers, Networks, Security. You need at least 48 marks (40%) to clear this. This is your main challenge. Your CS background covers about 30% of it; the rest is EC (Electronics & Communication).
Strategy
Don't just aim to qualify — a higher score means better merit ranking. Spend 70% of study time on Part B (EC topics) and 30% on Part A. In Part A, maths and reasoning are faster to improve than GK.

🧠 Reasoning & Data Interpretation — 25 marks

Puzzles, series, arrangements, coding-decoding, blood relations, data tables and graphs. This is pure practice. The more MCQs you solve, the faster you get. Start with Reasoning for quick wins.


Tip: Do 20 questions every day in the Testbook app during lunch. Speed is the key here — aim for under 60 seconds per question.

📐 Quantitative Aptitude — 25 marks

Percentages, ratios, time-speed-distance, profit-loss, simple & compound interest, averages. You studied this in engineering entrance exams — it will come back fast with 1 week of practice.


Tip: Focus on the formula sheet, not derivations. Memorise shortcuts for percentage and ratio problems.

⚖️ Constitution of India — 15 marks

Fundamental Rights, Directive Principles, Preamble, important Articles (like 14, 19, 21, 32, 370), Parliament structure, President vs PM powers, Supreme Court, emergency provisions.


Tip: Don't try to read the full Constitution. Get a "Constitution for competitive exams" PDF — it's a 30-page summary of everything that gets asked.

🌍 Current Affairs & GK — 15 marks

Recent news (last 6 months), important awards, science developments, sports, national/international events, Gujarat-specific news. This is the hardest to prepare because it changes daily.


Tip: Follow one daily GK app (like GKToday) for 10 minutes each morning. Focus on last 6 months. Don't go too far back.

Part B — 120 Marks (Need 48 to Pass)
11 topics · Explained simply · Difficulty rated for a CS engineer
01
Electronics Components, Devices & Circuits
Hardest for CS
~20 MCQs

What is this?

This is the foundation of all electronics. Just like in programming everything is 0s and 1s at the core, in electronics everything is about controlling the flow of electrons. This chapter teaches you the tiny components that do that job.

Think of a diode like a one-way valve for water. Current can only flow in one direction through it. A transistor is like a tap — a small signal at one end controls a large flow of current at the other end.

Key sub-topics

Semiconductors: Materials like Silicon that are neither full conductors nor full insulators. When you add impurities (doping), they become N-type (extra electrons) or P-type (missing electrons = "holes").

Diodes: Created by joining P-type and N-type silicon. Allows current in one direction only. Used in rectifiers (converting AC to DC), clipping, clamping circuits.

Transistors: Three-terminal devices (Base, Collector, Emitter for BJT). Used as amplifiers or switches. Three configs: Common Emitter (most popular, high gain), Common Base, Common Collector.

Op-Amps: Integrated circuits that amplify difference between two inputs. Used in filters, oscillators, comparators.

02
Digital Electronics & VLSI
CS Familiar
~15 MCQs

What is this?

This is the closest topic to your CS background. Digital electronics is literally about 0s and 1s in hardware — the physical layer of what you already know from programming and computer architecture.

If you've done any bit manipulation in code, you already understand logic gates. AND gate = && operator. OR gate = || operator. NOT gate = ! operator. It's exactly the same logic, just in hardware.

Key sub-topics

Number Systems: Binary, Octal, Hexadecimal, BCD conversions. You'll know most of this from CS.

Logic Gates & Boolean Algebra: AND, OR, NOT, NAND, NOR, XOR. Simplifying expressions using De Morgan's theorem, Karnaugh Maps (K-Maps).

Combinational Circuits: Adders, Multiplexers, Decoders, Encoders — circuits where output depends only on current inputs.

Sequential Circuits: Flip-flops (SR, JK, D, T), Counters, Registers — circuits that have memory (output depends on past inputs too).

VLSI & FPGA: How millions of transistors are packed on a chip. FPGA = a programmable chip. VHDL is the language used to program it (like C for hardware).

03
Electronics Networks, Instruments & Measurements
Moderate for CS
~12 MCQs

What is this?

This covers how electronic circuits are analysed mathematically, and the physical instruments used to measure electrical signals. Think of it as the "testing and measurement" chapter.

Network theorems are like shortcuts. Instead of solving a complex circuit from scratch, Thevenin's theorem lets you replace the whole circuit with just one voltage source + one resistor. Same answer, way simpler math.

Key sub-topics

Network Theorems: Thevenin's, Norton's, Superposition — methods to simplify complex circuits. For MCQs, memorise what each theorem states and when to apply it, not full derivations.

Resonance Circuits: When a circuit's inductive and capacitive reactance cancel out. Used in radio tuners to pick a specific frequency.

Transmission Lines: Cables that carry high-frequency signals. Key concepts: impedance matching, standing waves, reflections.

Instruments: Oscilloscope (visualises voltage waveforms), Signal Generators, Frequency Counters, Bridges (for measuring resistance/capacitance), Transducers (converts physical quantity to electrical signal).

04
Communication Engineering
Core EC — Study Hard
~20 MCQs

What is this?

This is THE most important topic for this exam. Communication engineering is about how information (voice, data, video) is sent from one place to another — through air (wireless) or cables. This is literally what the PSI Wireless job is about.

Imagine your voice is a slow moving river (low frequency). Radio waves are a fast highway (high frequency). Modulation is the act of loading your slow river onto the fast highway so it can travel far. At the destination, demodulation removes your river from the highway.

Key sub-topics

Modulation Types: AM (Amplitude Modulation) — strength of the wave changes. FM (Frequency Modulation) — frequency of the wave changes (less noise, better quality). PM (Phase Modulation). For MCQs, know bandwidth formulas and advantages of each.

SSB (Single Sideband): A more efficient version of AM — sends only one sideband instead of two, using less bandwidth and power. Used in long-range communication.

Digital Modulation: ASK (Amplitude Shift Keying), FSK (Frequency Shift Keying), PSK (Phase Shift Keying) — digital versions of AM/FM/PM. Used in WiFi, mobile networks.

Multiplexing: FDM (Frequency Division Multiplexing) — each user gets a different frequency slice. TDM (Time Division Multiplexing) — each user gets a different time slot. Used to send many signals through one channel.

Antennas: Convert electrical signals to radio waves and back. Key terms: gain, directivity, polarisation, half-wave dipole, Yagi antenna.

Telephone Networks: How calls are routed through PSTN. Switching types — circuit switching (dedicated path) vs packet switching (data in packets).

05
Communication Applications (Microwave, Fibre, Mobile, TV)
Heavy but Conceptual
~20 MCQs

What is this?

This is the applied version of communication — how the theory from Topic 4 is used in real-world systems like satellite TV, fibre internet, mobile phones and broadcast television.

Microwave Engineering

Microwaves are very high-frequency radio waves (1GHz–300GHz). Used in RADAR, satellite links, point-to-point communication. Key devices: Klystron (power amplifier), Magnetron (in microwave ovens and RADAR).

RADAR is like shouting into a cave and timing how long the echo takes to come back. The time × speed of light ÷ 2 = distance of the object.

Fibre Optics

Instead of electricity, fibre uses light to carry signals. Light travels through a glass fibre by bouncing off the walls (total internal reflection). Advantages: very high bandwidth, no electromagnetic interference, long distances. Three types of fibre: Single-mode (thin, long distance), Multimode step-index, Multimode graded-index.

Mobile Communication

GSM (2G): Voice calls digitised and sent in time slots. CDMA: All users share same frequency but with different codes. 4G LTE: Uses OFDM modulation, all-IP network, much higher speeds. Key concept: Cell towers divide a city into "cells" — as you move, your call hands off to the next tower.

Television Engineering

PAL system (used in India) — how colour TV signal is created. Understand: composite video signal, colour burst, horizontal/vertical sync pulses. TV modulation uses VSB (Vestigial Sideband) for video and FM for audio.

06
Microprocessors & Microcontrollers
CS Background Helps
~12 MCQs

What is this?

The 8085 microprocessor is an old but foundational chip from the 1970s. It's to EC students what learning C in terminal is to CS students — old school, but teaches you how hardware really works. Your CS background will help you understand the concepts quickly.

A microprocessor is like a tiny brain that reads instructions one by one and executes them. The 8085 has an 8-bit brain — it can only think in 8-bit chunks at a time. Modern CPUs think in 64 bits. Same idea, just more powerful.

Key sub-topics

8085 Architecture: ALU (Arithmetic Logic Unit), Registers (A, B, C, D, E, H, L, SP, PC), address bus (16-bit), data bus (8-bit). Know what each register does.

Instruction Set: Data transfer (MOV, MVI), Arithmetic (ADD, SUB), Logical (ANA, ORA), Branch (JMP, CALL, RET). Don't memorise all — just the commonly asked ones.

8051 Microcontroller: Has built-in RAM, ROM, I/O ports — like a mini-computer on a chip. Used in embedded systems (washing machines, remotes). Key: it has 4 ports (P0, P1, P2, P3), 128 bytes RAM, 4KB ROM.

07
Computer Networks
Your Strongest Topic
~10 MCQs

What is this?

As a CS engineer, this is your home turf. You already know most of this. Just make sure you can answer MCQs confidently — don't waste too much study time here.

The OSI model is like a postal system. The Application layer is you writing the letter. The Transport layer is the envelope with address. The Network layer is the sorting office deciding the route. The Physical layer is the actual truck carrying it.

Key sub-topics to quickly review

OSI Model (7 layers): Physical, Data Link, Network, Transport, Session, Presentation, Application. Know which protocols belong to which layer (HTTP = Application, TCP = Transport, IP = Network, Ethernet = Data Link).

TCP/IP: Three-way handshake (SYN, SYN-ACK, ACK). Difference between TCP (reliable, slow) and UDP (fast, unreliable).

IP Addressing: IPv4 classes (A, B, C), subnetting basics, private vs public IPs. IPv6 — 128-bit addresses.

Network Devices: Hub (broadcasts to all), Switch (smart hub, MAC-based), Router (IP-based routing), Gateway, Firewall.

OSI LayersTCP vs UDPSubnettingDHCPDNSARPVPNHTTP/HTTPS
08
Essentials of Network Security
Easy for CS Background
~8 MCQs

What is this?

Security fundamentals — how data is protected during transmission. If you've ever used HTTPS or heard of SSL, you already know the basics. This topic is very scoring for CS engineers.

Symmetric encryption is like both people having the same key to a lock — fast, but you have to secretly share the key first. Asymmetric encryption is like a padlock anyone can close (public key), but only one person has the key to open it (private key).

Key sub-topics

Symmetric Encryption: Same key for encrypt and decrypt. Examples: AES, DES. Fast but key distribution is a problem.

Asymmetric Encryption: Public key + Private key pair. Examples: RSA. Slower but solves key sharing problem. Used in HTTPS.

Basic Arithmetics in Encryption: Modular arithmetic, prime numbers — the math behind RSA. For MCQs, understand the concept, not the proofs.

OS Security: Authentication, access control, firewalls, intrusion detection.

AESDESRSADigital SignatureHash FunctionSSL/TLSFirewallIDS
09
Web Technology
Quick Win
~8 MCQs

What is this?

This is specifically about ASP.NET and web development concepts. A bit old-school (ASP.NET Classic / ASP.NET Web Forms), but the exam focuses on conceptual knowledge, not coding. Very easy scoring for CS engineers.

Key sub-topics

ASP.NET vs Classic ASP: ASP.NET is Microsoft's web framework built on .NET. Classic ASP was the older version using VBScript. Key differences: ASP.NET is compiled (faster), supports code-behind, has better security.

Client-Server Architecture: Browser (client) sends HTTP request → Web server processes it → sends back HTML response. Know the difference between client-side (runs in browser: JavaScript, HTML) and server-side (runs on server: C#, PHP).

Web Application Components: IDE (Visual Studio), Solution/Project structure, Web.config file, Global.asax, Code-behind files (.aspx.cs).

Visual Studio: Know the basic IDE components: Toolbox, Solution Explorer, Properties window, Code editor.

10
Android Application Development
Conceptual — Don't Over Study
~8 MCQs

What is this?

Basic Android development concepts. MCQs here are mostly conceptual — they test if you know what Android is and how apps are structured, not how to code them.

An Android Activity is like a single screen in an app. When you open WhatsApp, the home screen is one Activity. When you open a chat, it's another Activity. They stack like a pile of cards.

Key sub-topics

Android Architecture: Linux Kernel at bottom → Libraries + Android Runtime → Application Framework → Applications at top. Android apps run in the Dalvik/ART virtual machine.

Activity Lifecycle: onCreate() → onStart() → onResume() → [Running] → onPause() → onStop() → onDestroy(). For MCQs, know which method is called when (e.g., when user presses Home button, onPause then onStop is called).

UI Components: Activity (screen), Fragment (part of screen), Intent (message to start Activity), Layout files (XML-based UI), Views (Button, TextView, EditText), Adapters (for lists).

SQLite Database: Android's built-in database for local storage. Key classes: SQLiteDatabase, ContentValues, Cursor.

11
Current Trends (4G LTE, POLNET, IoT, Surveillance)
Read & Remember
~5 MCQs

What is this?

This is a "general awareness about modern tech" chapter. Since this is a Police (PSI) exam, some topics are directly related to police communication systems used in Gujarat. Light on theory, heavy on factual recall.

Key sub-topics

4G LTE: Long Term Evolution. Uses OFDM modulation. All-IP network — even voice calls go over internet protocol (VoLTE). Peak speeds: 100 Mbps download. India's major 4G providers: Jio, Airtel, Vi.

POLNET: This is Gujarat Police's own communication network. It's a dedicated wireless network for police. Understand the concept — why police need their own network (security, reliability, priority access).

Disaster Management Communication: How communication systems are designed to survive disasters. Concepts: satellite backup, mesh networks, redundancy.

Real Time Surveillance: CCTV networks, IP cameras, video analytics, central monitoring systems. Concepts relevant to police work.

Recent Advancements: 5G concepts, IoT (Internet of Things), cloud communication, SDR (Software Defined Radio).

Strategy & Exam Tips
These will save marks on exam day
🎯

Qualify both parts separately. 40% in each part is mandatory to appear in the merit list. Don't ignore Part A (aptitude) just because Part B (technical) has more marks. A 39% in Part A disqualifies you even if you score 100% in Part B.

⚠️

Use the "E – Not Attempted" option wisely. If you're genuinely unsure, mark E. You lose nothing. But if you can eliminate 2 options, statistically it's worth attempting — your net expected score is positive. Never leave something blank without selecting E, as unattempted answers also get -0.25.

⏱️

Time management in the hall. 200 questions in 3 hours = 54 seconds per question. Move fast on easy ones. Mark tough ones for review. Don't spend more than 90 seconds on any single question during the first pass.

🧠

Start with your strongest topics. In Part B, do Computer Networks, Network Security, Android and Web first — these are your CS strength areas. Build confidence and score early, then tackle EC topics.

📱

For EC MCQs, focus on concepts not formulas. MCQ exams rarely ask you to calculate bandwidth from scratch. They ask things like "which modulation has the widest bandwidth?" or "what is the advantage of FM over AM?". Understand the why, not just the numbers.

🔄

Spaced repetition is your best friend. Don't study a topic once and move on. Every Sunday, spend 30 minutes re-reading notes from the previous week. This alone will double your retention by exam day.

🌙

The night before the exam. Don't study any new topics. Just lightly re-read your own notes. Sleep by 10:30pm. Your brain consolidates memory during sleep — a rested brain outperforms a cramming brain every time.

💡

For Constitution questions, the most asked topics are: Fundamental Rights (Part III), Directive Principles (Part IV), Emergency provisions (Articles 352, 356, 360), Amendment procedure (Article 368), and the President vs Governor powers. Cover these first.

🔋

Don't sacrifice health for study. Your swimming, walking, and gym routine is actually an asset. Physical exercise improves brain plasticity and memory consolidation. Keep your routine. Just add 90 minutes of focused study at night.
